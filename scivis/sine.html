The function of sine and cosine



Then I remember the question of finding the height of a flag pole given a shadow length on the ground and the angle at the end of the shadow to the top of the flagpole. I felt that this is one of those problems that was created for an Algebra lesson instead of the other way around. Unless you have a clinometer, how would you know the 'angle at the end of the shadow to the top of the flagpole'? Even if you have one, unless your eyes can go lower than the ground, how can you orient it? I don't know. So I started looking at where else I can use these functions.

Sine and Cosine solve the problem of finding the position of an object that moves on a circular path at a constant angular velocity as you can see right below. By relating object P to the angle theta, a position on the Unit Circle is assigned to theta. The x-coordinate of P is the cosine of theta while the y-coordinate of P is referred to as the sine of theta.

As the velocity at which P moves is not changing, this is a very basic application (I'll tweak this one later and hopefully achieve something interesting). Sinusoids, however, can be used to model certain natural behaviours. Sinusoid as a function can be written this way:

S(x) = Asin(wx + phi) + B for w > 0


where

A
amplitude, the farthest distance that the sine wave strays from its baseline
w
angular frequency, (usually denoted by omega but I'm a bit lazy), associated to the period of the wave
T = 1/f = 2PI / w
period, the length of time that a sinusoid can complete one cycle; w is the number of cycles completed over an interval of length 2PI
phi
phase, tells us what angle phi corresponds to t=0; how much the sinusoid leads or lags over the unshifted sine function; this is relative

As an example, based on the website of the US Naval Observatory, the daylight hours that Seattle, Washington's largest city, received on the first day of each month is tabulated below.

Before finding a sinusoid which models these numbers, let's plot the data. Hover your mouse over the bars, which goes from January to December, to see the values. Axes are not shown.


Tracing the peaks of the bars, the data is close to a sinusoid. However, fitting a sinusoid to the data is not an exact science. Constants A, w, phi and B must be calculated so that the function S(t) = A*sin(wt + phi) + B closely resembles the data. S(t) refers to the Seattle's daylight hours received on the first day of every month, 2013.

B determines the starting point. Regular sinusoids will have a vertical shift that is the average of the minimum and maximum values. Based on the data, B = (8.5 +15.9)/2 = 12.2.

We then find the amplitude A. This is the displacement from the starting point to the minimum and maximum values. A = 15.9 - 12.2 = 3.7.

We now plugin values of A and B to S(t) which will give us 3.7 * sin(wt + phi) + 12.2.

Next step is to find the angular frequency w. Since the data is collected over a year, the period T is 12 months. w is related to the period by w = 2PI / T. Hence, angular frequency is PI/6.

The final quantity to find is the phase phi. As A is greater than 0, the phase shift corresponds to the first value of t with Seattle(t) = 12.2. April's 12.9 is closer to 12.2 than the previous value 11.1. Thus, our t is 4 making our -phi/w = 4; so, phi = -4w = -4(PI/6) = -2PI/3. Feeding this in our original equation this gives us

S(t) = 3.7 * sin((PI/6)*t - (2PI/3)) + 12.2.
